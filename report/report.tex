\begin{abstract}
We address ordinal sentiment classification of bilingual (German/English)
product reviews, where each review is mapped to a star rating in
$\{0,\dots,4\}$ and predictions are scored by mean absolute error (MAE).
The task is harder than standard classification because errors are
graded: confusing a one-star review with a five-star one is four times
as costly as confusing it with a two-star one. We compare three text
representations of increasing richness---sparse TF--IDF, frozen
contextual embeddings, and fine-tuned multilingual transformers---and
show that task adaptation, rather than scale, is the decisive factor on
commodity hardware. We further introduce a metric-aware decoding rule
that replaces $\arg\max$ with the median of the predictive distribution,
and a diversity ensemble of two architecturally distinct multilingual
encoders. Together these reach a validation score of
$\boldsymbol{0.908}$ ($95\%$ bootstrap CI $[0.907, 0.910]$), above the
strong reference, while training only base-size models on a single
$11$\,GB GPU.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Star ratings inferred from product reviews are a long-studied signal in
opinion mining, but the present setting combines three properties that
together make it non-trivial. First, the corpus is \emph{bilingual}:
reviews are split almost evenly between German and English, so a
monolingual approach forfeits half the data. Second, predictions are
scored by mean absolute error on a five-point scale, making the task
\emph{ordinal}---one-versus-five errors are four times as costly as
one-versus-two errors, unlike standard classification. Third, the text
is \emph{noisy}: it contains typos, emojis, repeated characters
(e.g.\ \textit{sooooo}), sarcasm, and reviews whose body contradicts
their title.

A natural question is which representation of the text best captures
sentiment under these constraints, and whether the gains compound with
metric-aware decoding and model combination. We answer it empirically by
comparing sparse lexical features, frozen contextual embeddings, and
two fine-tuned multilingual transformers under an identical evaluation
protocol; by deriving the Bayes-optimal point estimator under absolute
error and applying it uniformly; and by ensembling architecturally
diverse encoders rather than scaling a single one, motivated by the
numerical instability of large-model training in half precision on the
available hardware.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item A controlled three-rung comparison (sparse $\to$ frozen
    contextual $\to$ fine-tuned) on a single canonical split, isolating
    \emph{task adaptation} as the dominant performance factor
    (\cref{sec:methods,sec:results}).
    \item An MAE-optimal decoding rule applied to every model:
    replacing the modal class by the median of the predictive
    distribution lowers MAE at zero training cost
    (\cref{sec:decoding,sec:ablation}).
    \item A diversity ensemble of XLM-RoBERTa-base
    \citep{conneau2020xlmr} and mDeBERTa-v3-base
    \citep{he2023debertav3} that outperforms either model alone, is
    stable in fp16, and avoids the half-precision instabilities of
    XLM-R-large on Turing-class GPUs (\cref{sec:ensemble,sec:discussion}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Data and Evaluation}
\label{sec:data}
The training set comprises $n_{\text{train}}=252{,}000$ labeled reviews
and the test set $n_{\text{test}}=168{,}000$. The five classes are
\emph{exactly balanced} at $50{,}400$ each, so we use unweighted losses
and report accuracy alongside MAE without re-weighting. All model
selection uses a single $10\%$ stratified hold-out split of the training
set with seed $42$ ($25{,}200$ reviews); we report all numbers on this
held-out split and never tune on the public leaderboard.

\paragraph{Bilingual composition.}
A lightweight heuristic (umlaut characters and German function words)
estimates $\approx\!52\%$ English and $\approx\!48\%$ German reviews,
with the language distribution uniform across labels (within $\pm 2\%$).
This motivates a \emph{multilingual} encoder over per-language models or
machine translation: each model sees all $252$K samples and shares
parameters across both languages.

\paragraph{Length distribution.}
Reviews consist of a short title followed by a body separated by a
blank line; \cref{fig:length} plots the word-count distribution.
The $99$th percentile is $161$ words, so truncating to $256$
sub-word tokens covers virtually all reviews and bounds compute.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/length_hist}
\caption{Distribution of review length in words, clipped at $300$.
The $99$th percentile is $161$ words; we use a maximum tokenizer
length of $256$, which covers $>\!99\%$ of inputs.}
\label{fig:length}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Surface cues are non-monotonic.}
The challenge description flagged punctuation and repeated characters
as informative. Inspection (\cref{fig:surface}) shows their behavior is
not what a bag-of-features model would expect: the frequency of
``\texttt{!}'' is \emph{U-shaped} in the rating, peaking at both
one-star and five-star reviews, while the frequency of ``\texttt{?}''
decreases monotonically as ratings improve. The first pattern is
particularly damaging: a model that latches onto ``\texttt{!}'' as a
polarity signal will confuse the two extremes---the worst possible
mistake under MAE.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/surface_features}
\caption{Mean count of ``\texttt{!}'' (left) and ``\texttt{?}''
(right) per review, by true star rating. The U-shape of
``\texttt{!}'' implies it signals \emph{intensity}, not polarity,
and motivates context-aware representations.}
\label{fig:surface}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Evaluation metric.}
Ratings are encoded as $y\in\{0,\dots,4\}$ and scored by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:score}
L(\hat{\mathbf{y}},\mathbf{y})
  = 1-\tfrac{1}{4n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}|y_i-\hat y_i|
  = 1-\tfrac{\mathrm{MAE}}{4}.
\end{equation}
Errors are penalized in proportion to their ordinal magnitude. This
choice drives both our decoding rule (\cref{sec:decoding}) and our
emphasis on avoiding extreme misclassifications.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Baselines}
\label{sec:baselines}
\textbf{B1: TF--IDF + logistic regression.} Each review is mapped to a
sparse vector of unigram and bigram TF--IDF features
($50{,}000$ features, sublinear term frequency, document-frequency
threshold $\geq\!2$). A multinomial logistic regression
($\ell_2$-regularized, $C{=}1$) is trained directly on this vector.
This captures the most discriminative tokens (e.g.\ \textit{terrible},
\textit{perfekt}, and the literal phrase \textit{one star}) but
discards word order and context.

\textbf{B2: Frozen multilingual encoder + LogReg.} Each review is
encoded by a frozen multilingual sentence transformer
(\texttt{paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2},
\citealt{reimers2019sbert}, $384$ dimensions). The same logistic
regression is trained on these dense embeddings---a \emph{linear
probe} that isolates the value of pre-trained contextual semantics
\emph{without} task adaptation, and contrasts directly with B1
(dense vs.\ sparse) and with our main method (frozen vs.\ fine-tuned).

\subsection{Fine-tuned multilingual transformers}
\label{sec:main}
The main models fine-tune a pre-trained multilingual encoder
end-to-end with a five-way classification head over the
\texttt{[CLS]}-pooled hidden state.
We use two architecturally distinct encoders of comparable size:
XLM-RoBERTa-base \citep{conneau2020xlmr} ($270$M parameters,
sentence-piece, RoBERTa pre-training) and mDeBERTa-v3-base
\citep{he2023debertav3} ($278$M parameters, disentangled attention,
ELECTRA-style pre-training with gradient-disentangled embedding
sharing). Both are trained with AdamW \citep{loshchilov2019adamw}
under identical settings: learning rate $2{\times}10^{-5}$, weight
decay $0.01$, linear warm-up over $6\%$ of steps, maximum sequence
length $256$, per-device batch size $16$, three epochs, mixed
precision (fp16) with gradient clipping at unit norm, on a single
NVIDIA RTX $2080$\,Ti.

\subsection{MAE-optimal decoding}
\label{sec:decoding}
Each model outputs a probability vector $p=(p_0,\dots,p_4)$ for every
review; a decoding rule maps $p$ to an integer prediction. The
choice of rule is metric-dependent.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:median}
For an integer-valued random variable $Y\in\{0,\dots,K\}$ with
distribution $p$, the constant prediction minimising the expected
absolute error $\mathbb{E}_Y[|Y-\hat y|]$ is the \emph{median}
$\hat y^\star=\min\{k:\sum_{j\le k}p_j\geq \tfrac{1}{2}\}$, whereas the
expected $0/1$ error is minimised by the mode $\arg\max_k p_k$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
$\mathbb{E}[|Y-\hat y|]$ is piecewise linear and convex in $\hat y$;
its right and left subgradients at $\hat y=k$ are
$1-2\!\sum_{j\le k-1}p_j$ and $1-2\!\sum_{j\le k}p_j$, so $k$ is
optimal iff both bracket zero, which is exactly the median condition.
The $0/1$ result is immediate from
$\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}\{Y\neq\hat y\}]=1-p_{\hat y}$.
\end{proof}

Since the leaderboard metric (\cref{eq:score}) is MAE-based, we decode
with the median for \emph{every} model. The rule needs no retraining
and accepts a small decrease in accuracy in exchange for fewer large,
costly errors---exactly the trade-off MAE rewards.

\subsection{Diversity ensemble}
\label{sec:ensemble}
We scaled up by training two architecturally distinct
\emph{base-size} models instead of a single larger one. Given the two
fine-tuned models with predictive distributions $p^{(1)}$, $p^{(2)}$,
the ensemble prediction averages their distributions and decodes with
\cref{prop:median}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ensemble}
\bar p_i=\tfrac{1}{2}\big(p^{(1)}_i+p^{(2)}_i\big),\qquad
\hat y^\star = \min\{k:\textstyle\sum_{j\le k}\bar p_j\geq 0.5\}.
\end{equation}
Averaging \emph{probabilities} (rather than hard labels) preserves
each model's uncertainty; combining it with the median decoder
ensures the final integer estimate remains MAE-optimal under the
aggregated distribution. This choice is motivated empirically: an
attempt to scale a single encoder to XLM-R-large diverged under fp16
training on Turing-class GPUs (loss climbed above its random-init
value, gradient norms exceeded $500$), while wider-range
\texttt{bfloat16} is unsupported on those cards and full-precision
training of a $560$M-parameter model exceeds the available
$11$\,GB of VRAM. The two-base-model ensemble achieves a comparable
effective capacity at a fraction of the memory footprint and with no
numerical issues (\cref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Main comparison}
\label{sec:main-comparison}

$0.90$; mDeBERTa-v3-base alone already outperforms XLM-R-base by

\subsection{Error structure}
\label{sec:error-structure}
\cref{fig:confusion} shows the row-normalised confusion matrix of the
ensemble's median predictions on the validation set. The diagonal is
dominant ($66.6\%$ of all predictions are exact), and essentially all
remaining mass is on the immediate sub- and super-diagonal: off-by-one
errors account for $30.5\%$ of predictions, while off-by-three and
off-by-four errors together account for less than $0.4\%$. The most
costly confusions under MAE (one-star $\leftrightarrow$ five-star)
occur for $0.04\%$ of validation reviews---fewer than one in two
thousand.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Frozen contextual embeddings underperform sparse lexical features.}
B2, which uses a pre-trained multilingual sentence transformer as a
frozen feature extractor, scores below B1 ($-0.032$). The explanation
is that the encoder was pre-trained for \emph{paraphrase similarity},
not sentiment: it places ``great product'' and ``decent product'' close
together in embedding space even though they predict different
ratings. TF--IDF instead exposes the most discriminative tokens (and
even literal phrases like \textit{drei sterne}) directly to the linear
classifier. This is not evidence against contextual representations,
but against using them \emph{frozen}: once we fine-tune the same
family of models (\cref{sec:main}) we gain $\geq 0.05$ in score over
B1, and a further $0.001$--$0.003$ from ensembling.

\paragraph{Task adaptation dominates scale on this hardware.}
We attempted to push performance with a single larger encoder
(XLM-R-large, $560$M parameters) but found it impractical on the
available GPUs: half-precision training diverged
(loss climbed above the random-initialisation value of
$\log 5\approx 1.61$ and gradient norms exceeded $500$); the
wider-range \texttt{bfloat16} format is unsupported on the available
Turing cards; and full-precision training of a $560$M-parameter model
with AdamW exceeds $11$\,GB of VRAM. Rather than fight these
constraints, we obtained a comparable gain by ensembling two
architecturally diverse base encoders---a strictly cheaper and more
stable route to the same effective capacity.

\paragraph{Failure modes.}
Inspecting the rare extreme confusions, they are dominated by
sarcastic or internally contradictory reviews (e.g.\ praise of the
product followed by complaint about shipping) and by very short
reviews where the title and body provide conflicting signals. These
are genuinely ambiguous even to a human reader and place a natural
ceiling on this metric. We did not attempt to model sarcasm
explicitly; doing so is a natural extension.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
All numbers are reported on a single $10\%$ stratified split;
cross-validation across multiple folds, or repeated training with
different seeds, would tighten the confidence intervals further but
were not feasible under the project's compute budget. The
ensemble uses uniform weights; learned linear combinations or stacked
meta-learners on top of the saved validation distributions are
straightforward to investigate but did not yield additional gains in
preliminary experiments.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:summary}
We studied ordinal sentiment classification of bilingual product
reviews under an MAE-based metric. A controlled comparison showed
that fine-tuning a multilingual encoder---not richer frozen features
or a larger single model---is the decisive factor on commodity
hardware, and that two metric-aware techniques add further gains at
negligible cost: median decoding matched to the absolute-error
metric, and a diversity ensemble that sidesteps the half-precision
instabilities of large-model training. Together these reach
$0.908$ on validation with a $95\%$ bootstrap confidence interval of
$[0.907,0.910]$, using only two fine-tuned base encoders on a single
$11$\,GB GPU.
